Background Information

What is a sinusoid?
Basically, it is a function of the form:
f(x) = amplitude * sin(angular_frequency * x + phase)
where amplitude is any real number, and angular_frequency is any real number, and phase is a real number between 0 and 2*pi

What is a complex sinusoid?
Euler's formula gives us the form of a sinusoid function whose range is the complex plane:
f(x) = coefficient * e^(i * angular_frequency * x)
where e is the exponential constant, i is the imaginary unit, coefficient is any complex number, and angular_frequency is any real number.

A complex sinusoid is more compact than a real-valued sinusoid, so it is simpler to work with. Notice above how the complex sinusoid formula does not need a separate phase parameter? That's because its coefficient is a complex number rather than a real number, so it can encode both amplitude and phase.

A Fourier series can represent any periodic function as a sum of sinusoids. In this application I call a sum of complex sinusoids a "composite" to emphasize the graphic representation that I give them.

Fourier analysis lets us represent any periodic function as the weighted sum of complex sinusoidal harmonics, that is,
f(x) = c1 * e^(i * x) + c2 * e^(2i * x) + c3 * e^(3i * x) + ...
where c1, c2, c3, etc. are the complex coefficients that are "weighing" each harmonic.
